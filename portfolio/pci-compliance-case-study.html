The bottom line: PCI compliance is not optional for any business that accepts credit cards. The cost of compliance is a fraction of the cost of a single breach. Verizon's forensic investigators have never found a fully PCI-compliant organization at the time of a confirmed breach — not once.

Who Needs PCI Compliance?

Any business that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data must comply with PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard). This includes:

  • Brick-and-mortar stores with card readers or POS terminals
  • Online stores accepting credit card payments
  • Restaurants, hotels, and hospitality businesses
  • Service businesses that invoice by card
  • Any business using a payment gateway, even if the processor handles the transaction

Size does not matter. A single-location coffee shop and a multinational retailer are both subject to PCI DSS — the difference is the level of assessment required.

The Cost of Non-Compliance

Monthly fines from card brands

Visa, Mastercard, and other card brands levy fines on the acquiring bank, which passes them to the merchant. Fines escalate the longer a business remains non-compliant:

Choosing the right payment integration can dramatically reduce your compliance scope.

PCI DSS 4.0: What Changed

PCI DSS 4.0 replaced version 3.2.1 in March 2024. The final wave of new requirements became mandatory on March 31, 2025. Businesses that haven't updated are already out of compliance.

Key new requirements

RequirementWhat It Means
MFA for all CDE accessMulti-factor authentication now required for all access to the cardholder data environment — not just admins or remote access
12-character minimum passwordsIncreased from 8 characters
Payment page script monitoringMust detect unauthorized changes to payment pages (skimming attacks)
Authenticated vulnerability scanningInternal scans must use authenticated methods
Network security reviews every 6 monthsFirewall and segmentation controls reviewed semi-annually, not annually
Annual scoping documentationMust formally document what's in scope every year
Philosophy shift: PCI DSS 4.0 moved from an annual compliance event to continuous security operations — real-time monitoring and ongoing accountability rather than a once-a-year checkbox exercise.

Common Mistakes Small Businesses Make

  • "We're too small to be targeted." — 43–46% of cyberattacks target businesses under 1,000 employees. Attackers specifically seek out small businesses because they tend to have weaker defenses.
  • "Our payment processor handles everything." — Even with a hosted payment page, the business is still responsible for network security, access controls, and SAQ filing. The processor does not make you compliant.
  • "We don't store card numbers." — PCI DSS applies to any business that transmits or processes card data, not just those that store it. If a card is swiped, dipped, or tapped at your terminal, you're in scope.
  • Flat network with no segmentation. — Every device on the same network means every device is in PCI scope. One compromised laptop gives an attacker a path to the card reader.
  • Shared passwords on POS systems. — PCI DSS requires unique logins. Shared credentials make it impossible to audit who accessed what and when.
  • Treating compliance as a one-time project. — PCI DSS 4.0 explicitly requires ongoing monitoring, semi-annual reviews, and continuous security operations.

Getting Started

For a small business, PCI compliance starts with three things:

  1. Segment your network. Isolate the card reader on its own VLAN so a compromised laptop or camera cannot reach it. This is the single most impactful step.
  2. Choose the right payment integration. A hosted payment page (Stripe, Square) drops your SAQ from 329 requirements to 22.
  3. File your SAQ. The self-assessment questionnaire is how you demonstrate compliance. Your payment processor or acquiring bank can tell you which SAQ applies.
